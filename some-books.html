Some Books and Authors

Over the past few years, I've revisited the same few ideas, books, authors, and Twitter accounts, and I thought I'd list them down here. A lot of serendipity was involved in discovering them, and I hope this helps someone the same way mentions, recommendations, and citations led me to find their work.

1. Nassim Taleb

Quantitative trader turned philosopher, Nassim Taleb's books have ideas with the furthest reach than any other collection of books I've read. Without feeling even a modicum of cringe, I can say that his books changed my life. If you ignore the attitude (or even enjoy it), it will help you understand risk, nonlinearity, complex systems, tail events, the fragility of prediction, top down vs bottom up, antifragility of tinkering, incentives (crucially disincentives), and more. My favorites are Fooled by Randomness and Skin in the Game. The Incerto is accessible in any order, but I recommend starting from Fooled by Randomness.

2. Karl Popper (The Philosopher of Science)

What makes something scientific? How does one distinguish Science from Pseudoscience? Much of society gets a somewhat distorted view of what Science is about, particularly from famous science communicators who are prone to what can best be described as Scientism. Popper's ideas also changed how I think. It was eye-opening to realize that there is no 'true' theory, just theories that haven't been falsified yet, and the hallmark of a scientific theory is if it makes falsifiable predictions. A failed attempt at falsification makes a theory stronger.

For the general reader, I recommend The Open Society and Its Enemies. And if this reminds you of the Soros foundation with the same name, it might interest you to know Soros studied philosophy under Karl Popper. Ironically, the Soros foundation is completely Anti-Popperian.

3. Sean McClure (@sean_a_mcclure on Twitter)

He is less famous than the previous two, but I am glad I discovered him on Twitter. Valuable insights that have complexity as their primary theme. Highly recommend Discovered Not Designed, although reading Taleb first as a primer might be a good idea, because otherwise you might think he's crazy.

4. Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma and Related

Canonical Game Theory thought experiment—everyone should know it. Tit-for-tat + forgiveness generalizes very easily to all of life, and it will instill a desire to play long-term (iterated) games with people as a way to succeed. Unbeknownst to most selfish people, the optimal way to be selfish is to be selfless, so just be selfless and everyone wins. However, you can't do it with people that don't get it intuitively. The primary goal of business, then, becomes finding people you can play long-term games with, and cutting out short-term players.

5. Peter Thiel (Zero to One)

Thiel is probably the only insightful techno-billionaire. Packed with insights on startups, business, and sales.
